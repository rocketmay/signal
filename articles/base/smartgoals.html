An Introduction to Goal Setting

May Chan // September 16, 2022

Goal setting is a fundamental aspect of personal and athletic development. I wrote this article many years ago with a general approach, because this topic is so broadly applicable to any kind of endeavor. Just like shooting technique, there are many goal setting frameworks that you can use to improve. This article just covers one that I learned about and that I like in particular. What's most important is to understand why goal setting is important and use a method that works for you.

What is Goal Setting and Why is it Important?

At the most basic level, a goal is a desired result of an action or endeavor. In some ways, everything that we do is based on a goal, no matter how simple or complex that action or sequence of actions is. Many people pursue goals incidentally — that is, they don't specifically set goals or if they do they don't purposefully work towards them. Many people are on autopilot, floating around in the chaos of events that is life, reacting to what happens and dealing with the outcomes as they come.

"Goal Setting" is a tool to reach those goals. It is much like the oar of a boat, and goals are a destination. Like an oar, you can splash away randomly at things and hope for the best, or you can set a course and paddle with intent. By brandishing this oar, instead of hoping that the currents will take you to your destination, you can take things into your own hands and make sure you get there.

This sheet is an introduction to goals for the uninitiated. Purposeful goal setting is very powerful, because while we all have things we want to do, we are all limited — by time, by energy, by money, and other incidental circumstances. Without goals, we float about at the whims of fate. Goals let us take up an oar and start taking control of our destiny.

Goals are a Moving Target

Goal setting can be scary. In fact, many people avoid setting goals because they don't want to think about the possibility of not reaching them. They believe that by establishing a goal they are simply setting themselves up for failure. This mindset, while perhaps comforting in some ways, is mistaken because goals are not (or should not be) about success or failure, they are about directed progress.

You cannot fail at a goal. A goal is a target, something that you desire, something that is worth pursuing. If you miss your target, you can simply recalibrate and aim again. Obviously, we all want to succeed the first time we try something, but the key to success is persistence, not success itself. Let me repeat this again, because it is very important — you can NOT fail at a goal. If the target of a goal becomes unattainable somehow, then you simply recalibrate, and set course for another location.

Goals and goal setting are tools for positive change. They are not inherently good or bad. Simply the act of having a goal does not mean things will be better or worse. Simply holding an oar does not mean you will go anywhere. But it makes your actions clear. Even if you have a destination that you don't know how to reach, setting goals will help you take steps to find out how to get closer to that destination and find ways to eventually get there.

And when you eventually get there — well, there's another destination on the horizon waiting for you. Goal Setting does not end when you succeed at reaching a goal. Life doesn't end when you make it rich, it simply gets more exciting.

Goals Set Priorities

Goal setting is not just about the target, it's about the importance of a target. After all, most of us have many things we want to achieve — We want to be healthy. We want to be rich. We want that new smart phone. We want to make friends with that cute girl (or guy). We want to become experts at our passions. We want that promotion at work. We want to be happy.

We can set all the goals that we want, but at the end of the day, we are limited by the resources that we have. Even if we had all the wealth in the world, we are limited by time. And so, setting priorities becomes an intrinsic and critical part of goal setting. What is important to you? Out of everything you want to achieve, what do you want the most? Second most?

In order to achieve that most important thing, what are the things you need to do? Which of those actions are critical, which ones have a time limit? Which actions feel good to do, but ultimately don't get you closer to your goal?

When you lay out your goals, you realize that there are things that you thought you wanted, but in the big picture you don't really care about. When you lay out the achievements that you really want, you can focus on the steps to reach them. You find that the actions you need to take become clearer. You find that achieving some goals help you achieve other goals.

Ultimately, achieving the things we want feels good. Goal Setting is the direct ticket to that paradise.

SMART Goal Setting

Now that we understand what goals are and why they are important, let's get down to how. How do we set goals? What can we do to maximize what we get out of them?

Just like oars of a boat, goal setting strategies come in many shapes and sizes. However, a tried and true method is the SMART Goal system. SMART is an acronym. It stands for:

  • Specific
  • Measurable
  • Achievable
  • Relevant
  • Timely

SMART goal setting provides a structure for your goals. It creates a roadmap to reach your desired destination. For complex goals which have multiple steps or milestones, you can apply the SMART system to each intermediary step, and thus create a detailed plan that will fast track you to reach your final goal.

When you make a SMART goal, it must follow the criteria laid out by the acronym. The goal must be Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant and Timely. At first, this may seem daunting, but with practice it becomes second nature.

As we go through the meaning of each criteria, let us start with an example goal, and refine it through the system. I will start with the goal "I want to not be unhealthy". Feel free to try with your own goal as you follow along.

Criteria S — Specific

This criteria lines out exactly what you want to achieve. The more specific your goal, the clearer your actions to reach it will be. "I want to be rich" is different from "I want to make a six figure salary" is different from "I want to make a six figure salary in the film industry as a director".

When you lay out your goal, be specific about it. Ask yourself defining questions:

  • What exactly do I want?
  • Where?
  • How?
  • Who do I want or need to be involved with it?
  • Are there any conditions or limitations?
  • Why do I want to reach this goal?
  • Are there any alternative ways to achieve the same result?

It's important that the goal is positively directed. You want to be moving towards something, not away from something. What you focus on increases, so if you focus on NOT doing something, you will be inadvertently making it increase. "I want to be rich" as opposed to "I don't want to be poor". "I want to be proactive" instead of "I want to stop procrastinating".

Example: My goal of, "I want to not be unhealthy" is vague, negative and doesn't give us much direction. But if I refine it a bit, I can come up with "I want to be at a healthy weight and fitness level". That already gives us the beginning of a plan.

Criteria M — Measurable

This criteria lines out how you are going to decide if you've reached your goal, and also how much you have progressed towards it. This involves breaking down the goal into measurable evidence. "Feeling" healthier is not enough. Cutting down your smoking from one pack a day to half a pack (and eventually, from half a pack to zero) is measurable. "Making more money" is not measurable, but "getting to a point where you can save $100 a month" is.

Making your goal measurable gives you a way to track your progress over time. It means you can check in periodically and see the effect your actions are (or aren't) having towards your goal, and lets you adjust appropriately. It gives you a concrete target to aim for so you know when you've 'made' it.

Example: In "Specific" we refined my goal to "I want to be at a healthy weight and fitness level." Through the Measurable criteria, I need to refine it further. Perhaps it is "I want to lose weight and reach a weight of 58kg, and I want to be able to run a marathon." Wait a second, I have two goals now. I didn't catch it when I changed my goal from "Not being unhealthy" to "Be at a healthy weight and fitness level", but adding measurements really defines these as two different things. This is a good thing, because now I can really focus on it.

Criteria A — Achievable

This criteria explores how realistic and also acceptable the goal really is to you. Look at factors such as how much time you need to invest, how much effort, and other costs. This is also a good place to evaluate your priorities. How important is this goal? If the steps to achieve this goal will interfere with other goals and aspects of your life, is that acceptable?

If you don't have the time, money or the desire you need to reach that goal, you'll have to find ways to get it. This is where you may need to get creative or come up with intermediary steps. Or perhaps you'll realize that this goal is not achievable for you at this time, and you need to downsize or shift directions.

There's nothing wrong with aiming high. If you aim to be able to do 50 pushups at a time but you only end up able to do 30, that's still pretty good!

Example: My goal of reaching a 58kg weight is pretty realistic, given that it is within the healthy range for my height (thanks google!). So I'll keep my goal the same. Running a marathon, on the other hand? After doing some research it seems that there are a lot of steps to being able to accomplish such a feat, and to be honest it's not really an important goal for me that I would invest the time and effort to reach. But running 5km without stopping seems like a reasonable goal. Doing some quick research, it seems that I'd need to build up to running 3 to 4 days a week. Also, I've discovered through my research that "5km" is not actually specific enough, because running speed is also a factor. So I'll update my goal to be "Be able to run 5km in 30 minutes". Both of these goals are achievable and also realistic, so this criteria is locked down.

Criteria R — Relevant

This criteria explores how relevant the goal is to you. How important is it to you to achieve this goal? It explores the why of the goal. Why do you want it? What will be the significance of achieving it?

This is where you ask yourself, do you really want to be famous? Do you really want to live in Italy? Or is something else more important to you? Perhaps you don't really want a porsche, you just want to be rich enough to own one — that porsche is more of a symbol of your real goal of financial stability. Or perhaps you don't need to take that vacation to Hawaii, because you really just need some time to yourself and you'd be happier to take your vacation in the comfort of your own home.

For intermediary goals (that aim towards a higher one), this is a particularly important criteria because things that you thought would help you get closer to your main goal might not actually be that vital. There's no point in spending hundreds of dollars on a fine dining french cooking course if you just want to cook simple food for yourself at home. You might not need to buy an expensive suit for an upcoming interview if your portfolio is more important in the industry.

Example: Being at a healthy weight is relevant to my goals of being healthy so that's fine. Being able to run a 5km in 30 minutes is also an important milestone for me fitness-wise. My previous goal of "being able to run a marathon" would probably also fail this criteria, but the "Attainable" category already refined it for me, and I'm happy with where it's at.

Criteria T — Timely

This criteria gives us a boundary to achieve the goal within. It is quite important, because goals that are never accomplished aren't useful to us. By setting a "when", the entire process is tightened up and put into frame.

This is the time to set deadlines to reach your goals, and deadlines for the intermediary milestones. Be realistic about how fast you'll be able to achieve your goal and how much time you'll be able to allot to get there (Go back to the attainable criteria). It's okay to be optimistic, especially if it lights a fire under you to start acting now towards that goal.

Most importantly, remember that this is above all about positive progression. If the deadline comes up and you haven't achieved your goal, you haven't failed. It's simply a cue for you to re-evaluate the goal and recalibrate your sights.

Example: Looks like I haven't set a time for either of my goals. I'd love to be at 58kg by next month or even sooner, but that would mean I'd need to lose an unhealthy amount of weight each week (the internet says, more than 1kg a week is unhealthy). Since my end goal is to be healthy, then realistically I should set my end date of 58kg to be 6 months from now. This also gives me the ability to set monthly milestones so that I can track my progress and make sure that each month I am making progress to my goal of 58kg. For running 5km, according to the internet I should be able to reach that goal in eight weeks if I train really hard. But that would mean training very hard, which is not as attainable to me based on the amount of effort I want to commit to this. So I will set my goal for twelve weeks instead, to give myself a bit more time to reach it. And since I have two metrics to measure my progress (distance and speed), I can set milestones for myself to check in every few weeks.

Summary

SMART Goals are an effective and powerful way to set yourself up for success, no matter how big or small your goals are. By framing your goals in this system, you give yourself an excellent tool to keep track of your progress and really hone in on the things that are important to you.

Goal Setting is at its best when you can make it a habit, checking in with yourself on a weekly or monthly basis and seeing where you're at. Being able to measure progress towards your goals is extremely incentivizing. Being able to concretely form a list of actions and then pursue those actions can take a lot of the guesswork and chance out of achieving.

Since you've read this, you're already well on your way to achieving great things! Give yourself a strong start by putting together a SMART Goal. It doesn't have to be big. It could be as simple as "I want to leave the house on time tomorrow". What's important is that you start thinking about your goals in ways that help you achieve them.

It can be very helpful to write down your goals as well. The act of putting pen to paper can really make a difference in making a goal feel real. As an activity, take what you've learned above and write down what your goals are for shooting.
