Training for Results

By May Chan

Regardless of how much time you actually spend training, the most important factor when it comes to improvement is to be effective with that time.

The three elements of efficient training

To train efficiently you need to do three things. You need to know where you’re going (your goal), you need to know what you need to focus on to get there, and of course, you need to actually train that specific focus.

Rationale

Let’s say you only had two out of three of those elements. What happens?

If you know your goal and you know what you need to do, but in practice you don’t focus on the necessary things, then how can you expect to reach that goal?

If you know your goal, you practice a lot, but you don’t know what you need to focus on, then you’ll waste a lot of time practicing things that don’t move you closer to that goal.

If you don’t know your goal, then regardless of what you decide to focus on and how hard you work at it, there’s no way to reach something that is not defined.

Set a goal, know how to get there, focus your training

These three elements are not trivial, nor are they obvious.

Many athletes haven’t set a defined goal. They want to ‘get better’ and maybe one day ‘win a competition’, but you can’t plan solid paths toward nebulous goals. Some athletes avoid setting goals, whether it’s from a lack of confidence, anxiety, aversion to commitment, or simply because they don’t think it’s important.

There’s a lot more to be said about setting goals, and that topic is out of scope for this article. But ultimately if you, as an athlete, want to become better at your sport, then you must set a goal.

The second element, knowing what to focus on, also deserves its own article. However for most athletes the answer is pretty simple – listen to your coach. A good coach will pinpoint the most important aspects of your sport that you need to focus on and provide guidance, drills, and advice to address those aspects.

For athletes who don’t have a coach, or who perhaps have a streak of individualism and want to pave their own way, it might seem tough to know what you need to focus on. Again, this is the topic for another article, but at the most basic level what’s important is that you have chosen something specific to focus on. Somewhat counterintuitively, it doesn’t actually matter what you choose to focus on. The act of committing to a specific focus changes how you practice, and that change, even for a random focus, is still far better than having no focus at all.

The third element - training what you’re focusing on - sounds like the most obvious, but it actually has some nuance. There are plenty of athletes who put in dozens of hours at the range, and yet don’t really focus on anything in particular. They believe that simply by ‘trying’ they will improve, and while this is true to some extent, most athletes want results fast, and even if you’re patient, nobody has infinite hours to practice.

Effective training requires a defined goal and focused training toward that goal.

By training this way, you can make the most of your time, money and energy.

Take a moment and reflect on your own training approach. Can you check all three boxes?
