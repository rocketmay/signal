Introduction to Training Logs

May Chan // May 26, 2026

The most critical high performance training tool.

Training logs are used by athletes at the highest level of competition across basically every olympic sport, whether it's hockey, kickboxing, football, and of course shooting. They are a tool that is extremely useful both for the athlete and for your coach, supercharging your training so that you get the most out of every session.

Since we are focusing on 10m air pistol on this site, this article will talk specifically about how to use training logs for this sport.

Training logs are a tool that every athlete should use. Full stop. If you aren't using a training log and you're serious about improving and competing at the highest level, you need to start keeping one.

Basic Rules of Keeping a Training Log

  • The sole purpose of a training log is to maximize the effectiveness of your training.
  • A training log is a physical notebook.
  • Every practice session should end with an entry in your training log.
  • Training log entries should take no more than five minutes to complete.

Basic Rules Breakdown

The sole purpose of a training log is to maximize the effectiveness of your training

Training logs are not a journal. They're not simply a record of what you did at the range. They're a performance tool that you specifically keep in order to improve your training performance.

A training log is a physical notebook

The act of writing down your thoughts and results on paper makes a much stronger imprint than any kind of entry onto a digital device. Do not use your phone or a digital device to keep your training log.

Get a small, cheap notebook and a pen that you can throw in your bag, so that you always have it. I like grid paper, because it makes recording series of scores very easy.

Every practice session should end with an entry in your training log

Like everything else to do with training, consistency is key. Make it a habit to write in your log before you leave the range. Ideally, you should write in your log before you pack up and leave the line. By doing this you are keeping your experiences and thoughts from practice fresh in your mind, maximizing the effectiveness of the exercise.

Training log entries should take no more than five minutes to complete

Training log entries should be short, quick and succinct for two main reasons. First, you want to only record information that will be relevant to your training. Second, making the process short makes it far easier to keep as a habit. Jotting down a few thoughts and numbers at the end of a long practice is easy to do, whereas if you feel like you have to write an essay every time, you're not likely to be consistent.

Once you're firmly in the habit of keeping a log you might find that you have more to write on some days, but at the start do yourself a favour and keep the entries short and sweet.

What to Write in Your Training Log

Here are some ideas for what to track in your training log:

  • Date of practice, practice start and end time
  • What your focus was for the day. This is a critical one. More on this below.
  • Any stand-out observations about practice that you think are worth writing down. For example, if you shot your personal best that day.
  • Write down the scores for your practice. You can put as much or little detail as you like. Shooting performance is easy to quantify compared to other sports.

Other things you can note down, if you feel they're relevant that day:

  • Your physical condition that day, just a few words. For example, "Tired", "Bad sleep last night", "Sore from exercise"
  • Your mental condition. "Relaxed", "Stressed from school", "In the zone!"

How to Use the Training Log

Now that you know why you're keeping a log (to accelerate your training) and what you're writing in it, let's talk about how to use the log.

The critical thing to understand about keeping a training log is that keeping a log is an active process. You are not simply writing down results, you are reflecting on your training, you are keeping track of what is working and what isn't, and you are using that information to inform your training in the future.

Reinforcing your practice

Every time you practice, you should be aiming to accomplish something specific with that practice. When you finish practice, taking the time to evaluate how successful you were at achieving your goals for that session will reinforce the results of that practice.

You've just spent an hour or two (or more) practicing — you want to retain as much of that effort as possible. You're also probably tired. All it takes is a few minutes writing down those thoughts in the log and you'll have benefited greatly.

Therefore, once you've written the date and time of your practice (15 seconds), take two minutes to write down your focus for the day, and consider also writing down how effective you think you were at training it. For example, you could write "Focus: smooth trigger pull", and supplement it with, "Feeling like it's better than before," or perhaps "Got distracted."

You really don't need more than that. It's that simple.

Maintaining focus across sessions

Now, the next time you show up at the range, how do you decide what your focus is?

Your training log can help with that. The simplest thing is to review what you did in your previous practice session and continue with the same focus. Working on the same technique or drill over a period of time will produce more results than constantly changing up your training. Since you are now logging what you are doing, you can be deliberate about your practice.

More than that, since you reflected on your training when you wrote your previous logs, you will develop insight into what is working and what isn't. You can hone the parts of your training that are effective and discard the ones that aren't.

Tracking your progress

As the weeks and months pass, your log becomes an invaluable record of your progress. It lets you notice trends. It helps you learn more about yourself: what types of training are most effective for you, what things (such as sleep, hydration, stress) affect your condition and performance, what mistakes do you keep making that you need to fix, or what are the beneficial things that you need to do more of.

If you track your score, you get to see your results and performance improve over time, and that can be a huge motivating factor on the bad days. Sometimes, you will go months without any change in your scores, but when you look at your logs from the past you can say without a doubt that the mistakes and challenges you faced back then are ones that you have since overcome.

Even if your scores don't reflect it, your training log gives you perspective that you would never get if you didn't have one.

Conclusion

There's much more to be said about training logs, but hopefully what I've laid out here is a convincing argument as to why you should be keeping one, starting today.

If you're serious about improving at this sport (or any other endeavor, really), if you want to get the most out of the effort that you put in, if you're determined to be the best that you can be…

Keep a training log!
